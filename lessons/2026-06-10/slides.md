# The buffet problem

<div class="space-y-6 max-w-3xl mx-auto text-lg">

<div class="p-8 bg-gradient-to-r from-amber-50 to-orange-50 rounded-xl text-left">

Imagine you're at a buffet. There are 20 dishes. You love some of them. Others don't appeal to you — maybe they're inconvenient, or you just don't feel like it today.

So you skip the ones you don't want. That's totally fine at a buffet.

**What happens if you approach God's commandments the same way?**

</div>

<div class="text-sm text-gray-600 italic">
Take a moment to think. Is obedience something you can customize?
</div>

</div>

# Who was Saul?

<div class="space-y-4 max-w-4xl mx-auto">

<div class="p-5 bg-blue-50 rounded-lg text-base leading-relaxed">

Saul was Israel's **first king** — chosen by God, anointed by the prophet Samuel, and filled with the Spirit. He had tremendous potential.

But a pattern began to emerge: when the Lord gave Saul specific instructions, Saul obeyed *partially* — keeping the parts he agreed with and adjusting the parts he didn't.

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-yellow-50 rounded-lg text-sm">

**Earlier example:** [1 Samuel 13:8–14](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/1-sam/13?lang=eng&id=p8-p14#p8) — Saul grew impatient waiting for Samuel and offered a sacrifice he wasn't authorized to give. Samuel's response: *"Thou hast done foolishly… the Lord would have established thy kingdom… but now thy kingdom shall not continue."*

</div>

</div>

<!--
Presenter Notes:
- This brief backstory is key — today's incident in chapter 15 is not a one-off. It's a pattern.
- The 1 Samuel 13 cross-reference shows the consequences were already accumulating before chapter 15.
- You don't need to read 1 Sam 13 in full — just establish the pattern.
-->

# The Lord's specific command — [1 Samuel 15:1–3](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/1-sam/15?lang=eng&id=p1-p3#p1)

<div class="space-y-4 max-w-4xl mx-auto">

<div class="p-6 bg-blue-50 rounded-lg text-base leading-relaxed">

Samuel delivered the Lord's command clearly:

> *"Now go and smite Amalek, and utterly destroy all that they have, and spare them not; but slay both man and woman, infant and suckling, ox and sheep, camel and ass."*

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-indigo-50 rounded-lg text-sm">

**Mark the verse:** How specific was this command? Count the categories the Lord explicitly named.

What would "complete obedience" look like here?

</div>

</div>

<!--
Presenter Notes:
- The command is uncomfortable — that's intentional. Don't shy away from it, but don't dwell on the violence either.
- The key observation is HOW specific and complete the instruction was. Saul had no room for ambiguity.
- Transition: "So what did Saul actually do?"
-->

# Saul's partial obedience — [1 Samuel 15:7–9](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/1-sam/15?lang=eng&id=p7-p9#p7)

<div class="space-y-4 max-w-4xl mx-auto">

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-4 text-sm">

<div class="p-5 bg-green-50 rounded-lg">

**What Saul did:**
- Attacked Amalek ✓
- Destroyed most of the people ✓
- Destroyed most of the animals ✓

</div>

<div class="p-5 bg-red-50 rounded-lg">

**What Saul kept:**
- Spared King Agag ✗
- Kept the best sheep and oxen ✗
- Kept the best of the fatlings ✗
- "Everything that was good" ✗

</div>

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-yellow-50 rounded-lg text-sm">

**V.9:** *"But Saul and the people spared Agag, and the best of the sheep, and of the oxen, and of the fatlings… and would not utterly destroy them: but every thing that was vile and refuse, that they destroyed utterly."*

</div>

</div>

<!--
Presenter Notes:
- The contrast is vivid: they destroyed the things they didn't want anyway, and kept the things they valued.
- Ask: "In what way is this similar to the buffet? What rule was Saul really following — God's, or his own?"
- Note: Saul "and the people" — this wasn't just him. Partial obedience can feel normal when everyone does it.
-->

# "I have performed the commandment" — [1 Samuel 15:13–24](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/1-sam/15?lang=eng&id=p13-p24#p13)

<div class="space-y-3 max-w-4xl mx-auto text-sm">

<div class="p-4 bg-blue-50 rounded-lg">

When Samuel arrives, Saul's first words are: **"I have performed the commandment of the Lord."** (v.13)

When Samuel presses him about the animals, Saul says the people saved the best animals *"to sacrifice unto the Lord."*

</div>

<div class="p-5 bg-gradient-to-r from-amber-50 to-yellow-50 rounded-xl text-base border border-amber-200">

**Samuel's response (v.22):**

> *"Hath the Lord as great delight in burnt offerings and sacrifices, as in obeying the voice of the Lord? **Behold, to obey is better than sacrifice**, and to hearken than the fat of rams."*

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-red-50 rounded-lg">

**V.24** — Saul finally confesses: *"I have sinned… I feared the people, and obeyed their voice."*

</div>

</div>

<!--
Presenter Notes:
- Three stages here: (1) denial — "I obeyed"; (2) rationalization — "we saved them for sacrifice"; (3) confession — "I feared the people."
- The real reason was peer pressure and personal preference, not a misunderstanding.
- "To obey is better than sacrifice" is one of the great doctrinal statements in the Old Testament. Invite a student to read it aloud.
- Transition: "Saul's justifications sound reasonable — almost spiritual. How do we do the same thing?"
-->

# President Nelson on picking and choosing

<div class="space-y-4 max-w-4xl mx-auto">

<div class="p-6 bg-gradient-to-r from-amber-50 to-yellow-50 rounded-xl text-base leading-relaxed border border-amber-200">

**President Russell M. Nelson:**

> "Picking and choosing commandments will not work. It will lead to misery. To prepare to meet God, one keeps all of His commandments."

</div>

<div class="p-5 bg-blue-50 rounded-lg text-sm">

**Think about it:**

- What commandments are people — or you personally — most tempted to treat as optional?
- What justifications sound the most reasonable?
- How do those justifications compare to Saul's?

</div>

</div>

<!--
Presenter Notes:
- Source: "Doors of Death," Ensign, May 1992 (or later reprintings). Confirm exact citation if needed.
- Saul's justification was even "spiritual" — saving animals for sacrifice. Our justifications can sound good too.
- Don't name specific commandments from the front — let students reflect privately or share what they're comfortable sharing.
-->

# The contrast: Jesus's obedience — [Matthew 26:36–39](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/matt/26?lang=eng&id=p36-p39#p36)

<div class="space-y-4 max-w-4xl mx-auto">

<div class="p-6 bg-gradient-to-r from-purple-50 to-blue-50 rounded-xl text-base leading-relaxed">

In Gethsemane, Jesus knew exactly what obedience would cost Him. He prayed:

> *"O my Father, if it be possible, let this cup pass from me: **nevertheless not as I will, but as thou wilt.**"*

</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-4 text-sm">

<div class="p-4 bg-indigo-50 rounded-lg">

**Saul:** Given a complete command. Chose his own version of it. Said "I have done it."

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-teal-50 rounded-lg">

**Jesus:** Faced the hardest possible command. Chose the Father's will completely. Said "not as I will, but as thou wilt."

</div>

</div>

</div>

<!--
Presenter Notes:
- This is the pivot from what not to do → what is possible.
- Ask: "What made Jesus able to do what Saul couldn't? (Trust in the Father, knowledge of the plan, love for us.)
- The contrast isn't meant to shame — it's to show that full obedience is real and it's what the Savior models for us.
-->

# Option A — Personal application

<div class="space-y-4 max-w-4xl mx-auto text-sm">

<div class="p-5 bg-blue-50 rounded-lg leading-relaxed">

**Write a scenario** — describe a situation where you might be tempted to disobey a specific commandment. Then answer:

1. What Saul-like justifications could make partial obedience feel acceptable?
2. How could those justifications eventually become destructive?

</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-4">

<div class="p-4 bg-white border border-blue-200 rounded-lg">

**[Mosiah 2:41](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/2?lang=eng&id=p41#p41)**

King Benjamin: those who keep the commandments are blessed in *all things* — both temporal and spiritual — and will dwell with God.

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-white border border-green-200 rounded-lg">

**[Alma 37:35](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/alma/37?lang=eng&id=p35#p35)**

Alma to Helaman: *"Learn wisdom in thy youth; yea, learn in thy youth to keep the commandments of God."*

</div>

</div>

</div>

# Discussion

<div class="p-6 bg-gradient-to-r from-blue-50 to-purple-50 rounded-xl text-left text-base space-y-5 max-w-4xl mx-auto">

1. **Why do you think Saul convinced himself he had obeyed?** What does his story reveal about how we rationalize partial obedience?

2. **What justifications do people use today** to avoid commandments they don't want to keep? How do they compare to Saul's "we saved them for sacrifice"?

3. **Mosiah 2:41 says the obedient are "blessed in all things."** How does knowing that change the way you think about commandments you find difficult?

</div>
